# for each pdf, read in the text to a new row in a dataframe
df = pd.DataFrame(columns=["filename", "text", "num_pages"])
for filename in os.listdir():
    if filename.endswith('.pdf'):
        doc = fitz.open(filename)
        num_pages = doc.pageCount  
        doc_df = pd.DataFrame({"filename":filename, 
                               "text":"", 
                               "num_pages":num_pages}, index=[0])
        doc_text = ""
        # get the text from each page in the document
        for idx, page in enumerate(doc):
            page = doc.loadPage(idx)
            page_text = page.getText("text")
            doc_text = doc_text + page_text
        # store the document text in the document dataframe
        doc_df["text"] = doc_text 
        # append the document df to the folder df
        df = df.append(doc_df)

# reset the index
df = df.reset_index(drop=True) 

# create lower case version of the text

While technology plays an important role in advanc- ing most of these goals, critical perspectives about technology are equally important to realize these goals in a sustainable way [18]. In particular, achieving Goal 8, which incorporates the agenda of promoting “full and productive employment and decent work for all”, will become increasingly difficult as Industry 4.0 becomes more prominent in the Global South [16]. The rise of AI and automation will keep replacing human workers with machines and robots and according to Bangladesh government’s Access to Information (a2i) project and International Labor Organization (ILO), more than six million people will lose their job in Bangladesh by 2041 as a result of automation, including 60% workforce of the ready made garment (RMG) sector, 55% workforce of the furniture industry, and 40% workforce of the agro processing industry [51]. Against these backdrops, it is imperative to study the past tech- nological breakthroughs that took place in Bangladesh to under- stand how under-represented and marginalized communities were affected and how they can be better protected in the future. Dur- ing the late 1980s and early 1990s, computers revolutionized the newspaper industry in Bangladesh by replacing the traditional hot metal typesetting technology [67]. The typesetters – also known as compositors – experienced a massive layoff as their skills were gradually becoming unnecessary in the new computer-led industry and they faced two options: adapt to new technology or perish. While there are a few success stories, the overwhelming majority of those typesetters gradually descended through the social ladder and went into oblivion. In this study, we interviewed 17 veteran newspaper workers (current and former) who were typesetters during that period to un- derstand their technology adoption dynamics. We found that when computer was first introduced in the press industry in Bangladesh, poor state of political, labor, banking, and legal infrastructures played an important role in marginalizing the typesetters. Although they initially saw computer as a magic tool, it eventually became a curse for them due to learning barriers, lack of opportunities, and hostile attitudes from the upper level management. We observed the emergence of a new precariat class among these dislodged type- setters. We also found that those who successfully coped during this transition period did so because of their individual initiative and received little institutional support. In this section, we first briefly describe the related literature on these two opposing human traits. Next we highlight the rele- vant works on Industry 4.0 and AI. Finally, we discuss the related HCI/CSCW/ICTD literature on technology adoption dynamics in the Global South. 2.1 Fear of New Technology The fear of new technology is primarily rooted in job insecurity and unemployment. In economics, the concept of creative destruc- tion is associated with describing the process of downsizing in a company or an industry after a technological breakthrough takes place, where previous skills are deemed unnecessary [73]. The fear of unemployment with the advent of new technology is an ancient phenomenon, which originated since the invention of wheel [82]. Many rulers from the past resisted technology mainly to preserve the employment of their subjects and reduce public outrage [78]. Rather than focusing on increased productivity, technology was often utilized for advancing public works that would help them gain popularity and safeguard political power [65]. After the First Industrial Revolution, however, there has been a paradigm shift in the relationship between technology and political economy [80]. The technological breakthroughs of the industrial revolution replaced rulers and feudal lords of the past age with private industrial capitalists who owned the means of production, including machinery and tools, and they started to gradually re- place human workers with machines for increased productivity and profits. As Karl Marx pointed out in his “Das Kapital”, when machines are employed in one branch of industry, it creates a re- dundancy of labor in other branches and results in a sharp fall of wages [61]. Friedrich Engels – the co-author of Marx in The Communist Manifesto – sharply criticized those industrialists as they ignore the perils of the workers and accumulate wealth on the misery of the mass of wage earners [62]. This trend persisted in the Western society during and after the Great Depression era. Beard argued that engineers and scientists are responsible for mass unemployment as their inventions were pivotal behind creating the situation [29]. In his works, Zachary demonstrated how the fear of automation returned as businesses began heavily relying on computers for the first time and machine tools slowed job growth on the shop floor [83]. Marxist scholar Braverman’s famous work on “deskilling” draws attention to the dehumanizing nature of routine work, which, according to his observation, had persisted since the birth of the factory system [35]. Our work reflects these arguments in the context of the Global South as our participants experienced a similar transition in the wake of computerization in the press industry of Bangladesh. 2.2 Embracing the new technology According to Joseph Schumpeter (who originally proposed the con- cept of creative destruction), in a capitalistic society, economic growth oscillates between contractions and expansions. With new innovations, older technologies are disrupted and a monopoly of the new technology is established, resulting in a contraction in the business cycle [73]. However, when more and more entrepreneurs adopt the new technology, it becomes less monopolistic and more competitive, leading to an expansion in the economy. He argues that creative destruction is an important trait of the modern capi- talistic society as it keeps the economic growth running through alternating cycles of contractions and expansions. Embracing the new technology, therefore, is an important phenomenon according to the views of the Schumpeterian model of growth. Many researchers have examined the technology adoption dy- namics in the Western world. In their empirical analysis, Bartel and Lichtenberg showed that highly educated workers have a compara- tive advantage in regard to the adjustment to new technologies [28]. In his study in a Western setting, Bessen showed that the new tech- nology often disfavors the ones who previously obtained the highest benefits with more skills and experiences with the current tech- nology [31]. According to his calculation, between 1979 and 1989, employment among typesetters fell from 170,000 to about 74,000, while their median wages declined by 16 percent, adjusted for in- flation [31]. Membership in the International Typographical Union fell sharply to the point where it merged with another union [31]. Despite acquiring a higher set of skills to become graphic design- ers, the typographers’ real wage actually declined and they had to constantly upgrade their skills by eventually learning web de- sign [31]. These suggests that keeping pace with the ever changing technology is a necessity for specialized jobs. In her important work, Morgan Ames brings the role of charisma in technology to the fore [25]. Through her analysis of the One Laptop per Child initiative and a survey of past charismatic tech- nologies, she not only demonstrates the ability of technology to evoke transcendental feelings but also describes how technology repeatedly makes unattainable promises [25]. She highlights the im- portance of technological charisma to resonate with local cultural values and identities [25]. With the rise of sharing economy [20, 33], and the penetration of “always-on” Internet in the Global North, tech industries revolution- ized the way of sharing, allowing people to share their idle items with strangers at a cost. Such ideas generated multiple industries such as Uber, Lyft, Airbnb, CouchSurfing, Zipcar, among others. 149 Technology Adoption Dynamics of the Press Workers in Bangladesh COMPASS ’20, June 15–17, 2020, , Ecuador These industries allowed people to use the service of an asset, which would be either owned by another person or business industry. This digital sharing economy platform was further extended to offer food delivery services and multiple start-ups like Doordash, Post- mate, and Grubhub emerged in this ecosystem of “gig economy”. Workers from all over the world, including Bangladesh, embraced this technological platform for its economic benefits and flexible work hours, which gave rise to local tech initiatives such as Pathao (similar to Uber) [5], HungryNaki (similar to Doordash) [2], etc. The understanding of this gig economy is important to explain the emergence of a social class called the “precariat” [77]. Due to lack of job security, members of the precariat class exists precariously, either by continuously searching for work or by being perpetually responsive to calls for gig work [77]. We argue from our findings that technology-induced job displacement creates new members of the precariat class who might become an important stakeholder in the gig economy in the near future. 2.3 Industry 4.0 and AI Industry 4.0, or the fourth industrial revolution, encompasses con- cepts such as smart factory and lights-out manufacturing [40], which would completely overhaul the labor market [70]. To formu- late a better policy to protect the marginalized employees from the automation boom of Industry 4.0, it is imperative to critically exam- ine the past industrial revolutions and their impacts on marginal- ized workers. In a pre-industrial society, slaves or serfs were in many ways the robots of modern time1. Since the Industrial Revo- lution, mechanization boosted productivity but it also put people out of work, lowered their wages, and diverted all the gains from growth to the owners of businesses [24]. As Industry 4.0 gets more matured [58], robots would threaten work, wages, and equality. According to a study, 47 percent of American jobs are at a high risk of being automated [46]. MIT economists Daron Acemoglu and Pascual Restrepo found that each multipurpose robot has replaced about 3.3 jobs in the U.S. economy and reduced real wages [19]. While the situation might not currently be the same for the Global South economies that still favor labor intensive technologies, the consequences would be more dire when Industry 4.0 eventually becomes a trend in these developing countries in the near future as significantly more number of workers will lose their jobs. Our work studies the past to provide recommendations to protect these marginalized workers. In their book, Brynjolfsson and McAfee argue that in contrast to the First Industrial Revolution, which helped make machines and labor complementary, the Fourth Industrial Revolution would make humans and machines substitutes [36]. In the process, workers with special skills or the right education will thrive because of their ability to use technology to create more values. However, workers with only ordinary skills will be replaced by computers, robots, and other digital technologies as those ordinary skills and abilities are being acquired at an extraordinary rate by machines. For this, first we take a look at the long-standing discussion around post- colonialism within social science, literature, political science, and other disciplines. Edward Said’s famous work “Orientalism” [72] first showed how the Western narrative of the Arab world is dis- torted, and how the panopticonic gaze of Foucault’s model of power [39] was based on an Eurocentric understanding of knowl- edge (re)production. Later, development scholars like Arturo Es- cobar [41], James Ferguson [43], and James Scott [74] extended this line of argument to the international development discourse and showed how development projects are often shaped by “for- eign” definitions of problems and solutions sidelining the traditional cultural interpretations and marginalizing the local communities. Scholars in the Subaltern Studies attributed this problem on the gap within the Western history and literature created by the absence of the voice of the poor and marginalized communities [76], especially from the Global South. Irani et al. have defined postcolonial computing as a lens to understand the technology transfer from the West to the Global South [52]. Inspired by this seminal work, a rich body of work in HCI/CSCW/ICTD and related literature shows how local people adopt foreign technology through a wide range of actions including repair [22, 50, 54, 71], recycle [22], hacking [23], non-use [49, 64], and breakdown [32]. We argue that computer in the late 1980s was a foreign technology in Bangladesh and it disrupted the prevailing economic, political, and social dynamics in the press industry by changing the mode of production. Our study critically investigates this phenomenon to offer recommendations for the upcoming in- vasion of Industry 4.0 to protect the marginalized workers in the Global South. 3 PRESS IN BANGLADESH Compared to English, Bengali typesetting is difficult due to its al- phabetical complexity [45], which made the early printing process costly, cumbersome, and limited. Unlike English, Bengali is not written in Roman script. For writing purposes, a complex Brahmi script is used and Bengali typesetters need to learn and memorize the complex procedure [67]. After the inception of hot metal type- setting for Bengali in the nineteenth century, the press started its journey in the region but did not become an extensive industry due to the painstaking typesetting process and lack of knowledgeable workers [60]. With the inception of Bengal Renaissance and growth of mass education [38], press became lucrative enough to run as a business and Bengali newspapers started to emerge besides the English ones. During the late nineteenth century, when Linotype was invented in the Western world, the press industry in this region was late to embrace the technology by a few decades [67]. Unlike the traditional hot metal typesetting, Linotype uses a “line-casting” technology 150 COMPASS ’20, June 15–17, 2020, , Ecuador Syed Faiz Ahmed, Pratyasha Saha, and S M Taiabul Haque that requires less time and workforce. Despite the convenience, Linotype machines were more expensive and although they were imported for a brief period of time, the technology never became popular in this region as it was in India [67]. The traditional hot metal typesetting continued to be the dominant technology during the late British colonial years, the provincial East Pakistan period, and even after the independence of Bangladesh in 1971. The first major technological breakthrough in the press industry took place when computers became popular in Bangladesh during the late 1980s [17]. The mechanically skilled typesetters – who were the driving force behind the typesetting technology – started to lose their relevance in the industry because their mechanical skills were being replaced by the digital skills of computer savvy younger employees. The traditional hot metal typesetting was a rigorous and labor- intensive process. In this system, letters cast on lead had to be set manually and the typesetters – who could set the text that reporters wrote letter by letter – on average could not compose more than two columns of text in a six to eight hours of shift. At least four typesetters were, therefore, needed to compose an eight-column newspaper page and as many as 32 typesetters were needed for a standard eight-page newspaper. A foreman oversaw the typesetting process and he had the copies proofread by proofreaders in pairs, who ensured that the handwrit- ten reports were replicated correctly. As all the typesetters had a day off every week and some of them could call in sick, an eight-page newspaper had about 50 typesetters and eight to ten proofreaders. Most of the media houses used to employ around 60 people or so in the typesetting department. In fact, the then-industry leader – the Daily Ittefaq – had more than 100 employees in this department. With computers, the demands for typesetters reduced by almost 75 percent as around 15 employees were needed in the computer section instead of 60, including the proofreaders. Against these backdrops, the typesetters experienced a massive layoff between 1985 and 1995. This study retrospectively focuses on those former typesetters to understand their technology adoption dynamics – how they survived, thrived, or disappeared. To properly understand this issue, it is important to recognize two key social and political factors. First, during this transition period, the job of a typesetter was symbolically important. On the one hand, it required literacy and had the aura of clerical jobs. On the other hand, it involved physical labor and the payment was not significantly higher than the hourly wages of the working class people. As a result, they consolidated as a buffer class with a high level of self- esteem but a low to moderate level of income. Second, this transition period coincided with a spell of political turbulence in Bangladesh when democratic parties there had been organizing a mass uprising to oust military dictatorship. The strength and the political power of labor unions were very crucial and played an important role in the adoption or negotiation dynamics of these workers. 4 METHODS Our interviewer (also a co-author of the paper) is a professional jour- nalist of a national daily newspaper who has been working in this industry for ten years. Through his professional correspondence, we started looking for veteran press workers who were employed in the typesetting department in the 1980s. We started by conve- nience sampling and interviewed those professionals who are still working in the industry. We found that very few typesetters from that period are still active in this industry as many of them have switched professions and some have already retired or passed away. The interview sessions with this group were interactive and vivid where we asked them questions about the evolution of typesetting in Bangladesh, their experiences as a typesetter, the challenges they faced after the adoption of computers, and the incentives they received during the transition period. We also asked in detail about their coping strategies and their overall sentiment about technology. They provided insightful facts and anecdotes that helped us sketch the scenarios from the past. After our first four interviews with this group, we started run- ning a snowball sampling and recruited participants from the rec- ommendation of our existing participant pool. With their help, we started looking for former journalists who had either switched pro- fessions after the technological breakthrough or had already retired. It turned out to be a difficult task to find many interviewees from this group as most of them gradually descended through the social ladder after losing their jobs and went into oblivion. Among the ones we found, a few were reluctant to share their stories while others were not much comfortable in the beginning but gradu- ally opened up to the interviewer. We interviewed this group with empathy and verbally comforted them throughout the interview, timeouts were offered as well if they were emotionally distressed. In parallel, we kept interviewing the typesetters from that period who are still active as press workers and asked them to describe the stories of their former colleagues who could not adopt the new computer technology. In total, we interviewed 17 participants. Three of them are now re- tired, one switched to garment industry, one became a stamp maker, and the rest are still active in the press industry, mainly as graphic designers. We could not find any female interviewee as there was no female typesetter in the press industry during that period, which has been confirmed by our interviewees. We had participants from English and Bengali newspapers, from government-owned corpo- rations and privately-held newspaper companies as well. Table 1 shows the demographic information of our interviewees. We did not provide any monetary compensation to our partici- pants but according to the Bangladeshi custom, offered them tea and snacks. The protocol for this study was reviewed and approved by the institutional review board (IRB) of the last author’s institution. All the interviews were conducted in Bengali and audio recorded with the prior permission of the participants. The audio data was then transcribed, anonymized, and translated into English by two members of our research team. The translated data was then an- alyzed by the whole team. It should be noted here that all the members of our analysis team are born and raised in Bangladesh and they are well-familiar with the culture of Bangladesh. We used the “Grounded Theory” approach [37] to distill various themes. According to this approach, we did not try to fit our data in any existing social or economic theory. Rather we used the “open cod- ing” procedure [30] and the “constant comparative method” [48] to reach theoretical saturation. We then describe the resistance of the typesetters in relation to the local power politics and highlight the local infrastructural issues that marginalized the typesetters. We unpack the psycho- logical dilemma of the typesetters who lost their job and became a member of the precariat class. We also report the issue of ageism in technology-induced job displacement. Next we highlight the success stories of the typesetters who were able to adopt the new technology during this transition period. Finally, we report our participants’ general sentiment about the upcoming technological breakthroughs. 5.1 The charisma of computers The computer technology came like a “magic” for the people back in the 1980s and our participants mentioned that they were enthralled by it. Papert and Negroponte – the masterminds behind the OLPC project in the Global South – effuse about the universal appeal of computer as it is capable to “take on a thousand forms” and “serve a thousand functions” [68]. We observed a similar mesmerizing power that computers had among our participants. One of them mentioned: “The computer transcends the human weakness. It is relentless, it provides maximum output, and yet holds the creative credentials, we were told. In a word, it was a sort of magic box, where we would ask things and it would deliver exactly what we want”. (P3) In particular, they thought that it would transform the painstak- ing manual process of typesetting into a creative task and provide a better work experience. They were prepared to embrace it as one interviewee mentioned: “Human civilization invented a new magic tool, we had to embrace it. We thought it would change the whole system and transform the laborious works into artistic fun”. (P9) However, they soon became skeptic about it as they were not sure if this “magic tool” could be maintained properly. There were not many computer savvy people at that time in Bangladesh and very few people had the knowledge about make-up of newspapers through computer. There was not much opportunity of mastering the technology either as there were no government institutions for learning computer and the few private institutions that used to train people were expensive and often did not have the capacity of teaching the skill of page make-up by using the appropriate software. One interviewee also pointed out the fact that there was no local market of computers and finding spare parts or repairing shops was very difficult. The typesetters were particularly appalled by the sophistication of the computer platform because they started feeling that they would never be able to use it properly. Most of them had little formal education beyond a high school diploma and they saw computer as a complex modern machine. There was a collective preconceived notion that the ability to use this complex machine is tied to the level of higher education one has, which they did not have. In retrospect, they feel that the charisma of computer was a repellent rather than a motivating factor for them to learn and adapt to it. One participant summarized this sentiment in the following way: “We were afraid of the machine. It was never going to be possible for less educated people like us to handle that. The sophisticated machine requires sophisticated, educated people. It was not our job”. (P4) Age was another important factor as the older typesetters were in a disadvantageous position to adapt to computers compared to the younger ones because their scope of utilising the new skill was very limited with very few years left in their job. Thus, the charisma of computers did not work well for those old, high-school educated typesetters. Instead, computer became a curse for them as they gradually started losing their jobs because of it. 5.2 Computer is the serpent The stories of the typesetters who were dislodged as a result of tech- nological unemployment portrayed a gloomy image. We highlight two of those incidents below. One of the participants we interviewed was relatively more fortunate than others because the top-level management of his workplace was more considerable and instructed to redistribute the typesetters in different sections of the office according to their capacity. However, the management could not accommodate him in a clerical job. Despite being one of the most experienced typesetters, he was given the position of a security guard. As both his salary and rank got reduced, he eventually abandoned his job. Another participant described his story of receiving a golden handshake package after losing his job. The payment amount was not enough to feed his family for many years, so he was looking for an investment opportunity. When he came to know about a lucrative business plan from another person, he decided to put the money into that venture. After a few months, he realized that the 152 COMPASS ’20, June 15–17, 2020, , Ecuador Syed Faiz Ahmed, Pratyasha Saha, and S M Taiabul Haque project was a scam, he fell into the trap of a Ponzi scheme, and lost his entire investment. We heard similar stories from other participants too. Many type- setters who failed to adopt the computer technology returned to their village and some of their young children could not cope with moving to a new environment. The migration affected their up- bringing and they were not able to recover from this. All these stories reveal that technology-induced job displacement has been a lifetime trauma for many of these typesetters. One typesetter described us his sentiment about technology in a very negative way: “Computer is the serpent that bites me, hurt me, and descended me to the lowest of hell. They say that new technologies are inevitable and we must embrace it for the sake of progress and things will be alright shortly. (P7) Some journalists we interviewed, however, were more neutral about technology and attributed their misfortune to a lack of proper impetus during the transition period. There was no clear path or vision from the management and decisions were more ad hoc in nature. In their opinion, those who survived did so because of their personal traits, not due to management interventions. 5.3 Resistance and Politics Although a few small, private organizations started to successfully implement the new technology by hiring new and young com- puter savvy employees, the industry leader Ittefaq and government- owned corporations such as Dainik Bangla (now defunct) and Dainik Janata faced resistance from the labor union. One of our interviewees presented us a vivid description of the resistance of the typesetters to the new computer-based printing technology: “During that time, the labor union of the typesetters was very powerful and they used to showcase their strength with a huge May Day rally each year. The people in this sector started to whisper about the arrival of com- puter and they started protesting inside and outside the offices. A popular slogan for them was: ‘We will shed blood but won’t allow computers’. The Press Club arena – an important hub in Dhaka city where many groups showcase their demonstrations and protest against so- cial and political injustice – became a regular meeting place for the disgruntled typesetters”. (P2) According to the veteran street vendors of that area, the demon- strations of typesetters for salvaging their jobs became a regular event. Even some government employees who were under the threat of losing their jobs and other benefits joined the processions hiding their identity cards. To counter this, the owners initially tried to exert force by hiring goons who got involved in physical brawls. One participant described us one of those incidents from his memory: “I can clearly recall that day when our nightmare be- came a reality and we finally received the notice of being fired. On that day, I was scheduled for the evening shift and when I reached office, I found that we were not allowed to enter the office premises as some goons were resisting us. We made some verbal and physical alter- cations with them and luckily our fellow workers, who were working in the morning shift, were inside the office and they joined us. We could eventually successfully enter the office that day.” (P1) This maneuvering period continued for a while when hired goons and the local police force were used to crush the resistance of the workers. However, the owners eventually adopted the age-old technique of divide and rule by making a compromise with the union leaders. It was alleged by some of our interviewees that a few union labors made a deal with the owners and acted as a double agent. One of them said: “In our office, a few top union leaders basically back- stabbed us. We were not against computers but we wanted to keep our job safe. These leaders told us that computers would be used experimentally to print only one ‘pata’ (one leaf/both sides of the page), but we were tricked. They started to print four instead of one and the number increased subsequently”. (P5) Some of those union leaders were also used as a bargaining agent for golden handshakes. They told the workers that the own- ers would compensate them if they resign. Initially, some of the workers accepted the package and were paid. However, when the number of workers got reduced and the protests became weaker, the owners started to provide bounced checks. One of the interviewees mentioned: “You know, these are big companies, no banks dared to touch them even if their checks were bounced. So, the cheated workers could do nothing. I know many people who never received the payment and died with the grief”. (P5) The owners also fired some of the influential union leaders who could not be bribed and when they went to court, they did not receive adequate legal support. The owners, who were politically, socially, and financially very strong, eventually won the legal battles. One interviewee frustratingly said: “This is a third world country, the law book states to save the hapless but it always favors the powerful ones”. (P5) These responses suggest that poor state of political, labor, bank- ing, and legal infrastructures played an important role in marginal- izing these workers during the transition period. 5.4 The Precariats The typesetters who lost their job became precariats from salariats. As Guy Standing explains, the precariat – a blend of the terms “precarious” and “proletariat” – is a social class whose members live precariously through uncertain phases of unemployment and un- compensated activities [77]. As a result, they fail to create a unified class identity and can never become united like the proletariat. The dislodged typesetters are a classic example of the precariats. They had two options: accept lower-ranked positions such as gate- keepers or peons in the same office, or quit. As highlighted in one of our above stories, many saw the first option as a huge demotion 153 Technology Adoption Dynamics of the Press Workers in Bangladesh COMPASS ’20, June 15–17, 2020, , Ecuador and left the office. In the highly segregated society of Bangladesh, most of these displaced typesetters – who took a great pride in their former profession as it required both literacy and manual labor – refused to adopt any new job involving manual labor only, as it was tantamount to losing honor and esteem in the society. One participant said, “My family, my neighbors, they all know me as an educated person working at an office, I could not afford to pull rickshaw. I would lose all my honor. I would rather starve”. (P4) Our interviewees said that many of these workers did not reveal the news of job displacement to their family members. They were in a hope to find a new job and everyday they left home pretending to go to office. One interviewee described the precarious life of his former colleagues in the following way: “They used to search some sort of job during the regular office hours and pass their time often sleeping on a bench in a public park before returning home, pretending that they were at office”. (P11) Some of these displaced employees got involved in many tem- porary gigs. They became street vendors, used their expertise to open a small business like stamp making, or temporarily switched to book industry where the old technology was still being used. Their social and economic degradation was evident and most of them left the city and returned to their rural homes. The fate of these migrating families was not good and few even got involved in criminal activities to change their misfortune. 5.5 Ageism We found that older workers get affected the most when a new tech- nological breakthrough takes place. One interviewee reported that he experienced a strong tone of ageism during his conversations about computer with the top-level management. He said: “I was told that computer is not for old people (above forty) like me. They also said that it is a modern machine and I will not be able to cope with it”. (P7) Aside from ageism, older typesetters faced a couple of other dis- advantages. First, as they spent more years with the old technology, the effort of re-learning new technology and spending fewer re- maining years of their career in working on it did not appear to be an attractive proposition to them. The alternative option – a golden handshake – appeared more lucrative to them as that came with a promise of a severance package. Compared to younger typesetters, the older workers were therefore more interested to retire with the package. However, as mentioned above, the owners basically tricked them by starting to write bounced checks after a while. Second, the union law mandated that between two willing work- ers, the golden handshake will be offered in a last-in-first-out pro- cess where the worker with fewer working years would leave first with a smaller amount of severance package. This essentially meant that older typesetters received the bounced checks as they retired later in the order and their misfortunes were the most extreme. 5.6 Adopting the new technology Having described the challenges and the perils of the displaced workers, we now focus on the success stories of the workers who successfully adopted the new computer technology. One of our in- terviewees described us his journey of becoming a graphic designer for newspapers in the computer era. He said: “Our owner was a very kind person. His instruction was to keep the job of all the existing workers to the best of their ability, even after the adaptation of computer. We were given chances and options but unfortunately, many workers failed to grab those as they were intimidated and thought that it would be an impossible task for them to master computer skills with very little formal education. But, I was adamant despite having very little formal education. I gave tremendous effort and time and ultimately that paid rich dividend as I thrived in the same office with my computer skills”. (P1) The responses of other successful workers also echoed the same sentiment. They identified two major factors that facilitated their adaptation: individual perseverance and kindness of the owner. All of them acknowledged that the scenario was chaotic as there was no clear institutional policy and luck played an important role in their survival. They remarked that workers who were not lucky enough to have a reasonable owner simply perished. These participants further described their long path to adapta- tion. One of them said: I used to get 4,000 BDT2 per month. My house rent was 2,000 BDT and I had to pay 12,000 BDT for a six-month long computer course. Luckily, I had the chance to pay in monthly installments but after paying 2,000 BDT per month for the training, I did not have a single penny and had to borrow from my friends and relatives in those months. However, many of my colleagues did not have this opportunity of spending 2,000 BDT per month as they had to feed their family members”. (P1) He also acknowledged that adapting to computer is a lifelong learning process: “The main challenge was keeping up with the pace of technology. I realized that the styles, techniques, and software were ever changing. Some were very enthusi- astic and learned the basic function of computers but failed to learn new things continuously. Ironically, when I started learning complex yet more effective software and other techniques in computer, my dynamic col- leagues – who successfully passed the stumble block of technological transformation from hot-metal (typeset- ting) to computer – started to feel that the technology is here to stay as a stationary phenomenon. In my opinion, people who are always ready to sail with the wind of change will survive and thrive in the long run”. (P1) These responses highlight that there are manifold challenges to adopt a new technology in Bangladesh and workers do not have control over all these factors. One important finding from our study is that people who managed to cope successfully with the transition period during the previous technological breakthrough are quite reluctant about the prospect of a forthcoming one. Their main reasoning is that in their lifetime, they will not see another similar leap in technology that they ob- served almost three decades ago. Their general tone about AI and Industry 4.0 is surprisingly underwhelming considering the fact that they have experienced firsthand how technological advance- ment disrupts the existing job market. For example, some of them believe that the major bottlenecks of AI will never be solved and machines will never triumph over human in the job market. One such participant said: “Robot will never replicate the job of a journalist. It can never be creative. A robot can never play sitar like Ravi Shankar. It can only reproduce mundane routine and pre-programmed work. The readers will easily un- derstand the difference between a good writer and a machine generated writing as the latter follows a pat- tern and lacks nuance”. (P9) In general, they were also very skeptical and not well-informed about the prospects of AI technologies. In fact, some of them simply laughed off the fact that the deep learning technology is surpassing all the imagination. A similar sentiment was echoed in the responses of the interviewees who currently work as graphic designers. In their opinion, machines will never be able to produce the “human feel” in design and the works of human will always be preferred over those of machines. 6 DISCUSSION In the sections above, we have presented a wide range of issues related to the technology adoption dynamics of the press workers in Bangladesh in the context of local culture, economy, and politics. These findings illustrate a nuanced portrayal of how technological breakthrough of computers disrupted the prevailing professional and social dynamics in Bangladesh in the late 1980s and early 1990s. Even after three decades, the political and labor intricacies of Bangladesh have not changed much [8, 9, 12, 42]. Our findings thus generate a number of policy implications and a few lessons for protecting the under-represented and marginalized workers in Bangladesh from the upcoming technology boom of Industry 4.0. We discuss these in the following paragraphs. 6.1 Educating workers Our findings are a microcosm of workers’ misery in Bangladesh due to technology-induced job displacements. For example, the ready made garment (RMG) sector, which is many times larger than the press sector in Bangladesh, will face a far more serious consequence once automation starts destroying millions of jobs in that sector [7, 8, 26]. In fact, there are a number of striking similarities between today’s conditions and conditions from three decades ago. The owners of the garments today are just as powerful as the owners of the newspaper corporations from that period were, as they hold a similar political and cabinet power [12]. In addition, labor unions are politically infiltrated and multiple banks are on the verge of collapse [8, 9, 42]. The power asymmetry between owners and workers is far more pronounced now compared to three decades ago. Without advanced planning and proper intervention from the government, there will be an unprecedented level of panic once automation affects the RMG sector. One important strategy would be educating workers about the potential consequences of Industry 4.0 and AI invasions in their life. Building on existing HCI research on SMS, IVR, and participatory video based interventions for education and awareness [56, 81], we suggest that the government reaches out to the general people. At the same time, there should be social awareness and protest against marginalization of the workers. The lessons learned from existing HCI and ICTD work around social movement might be helpful in this regard [21]. To unite workers and record their grievances, citizen-journalism has been demonstrated to be an effective tech- nique in the Global South. Communal surveillance might also be a useful approach to prevent chaos and disorder in extreme cases [84]. As technological change is exponential [57], it is quite natural that future workers might experience multiple technological break- throughs in their lifetime. Our findings show that even the workers who managed to cope successfully with the previous technological breakthrough are not mentally prepared to face the forthcoming one. Considering their age, it is reasonable that in future they will not be required to face a similar challenge that they successfully overcame almost three decades ago. However, it is highly probable that future workers in the Global South will experience multiple cycles of the Schumpeterian model of innovation and growth and they have to keep adapting to each of those to survive in the long run [73], which might become a significantly more difficult task. Any policy therefore should consider the importance of lifelong learning and the government should incentivize the lifelong learn- ing programs. 6.2 Rehabilitating workers The rehabilitation of the workers should be prioritized. As can be seen from our findings, computer training centers were expensive (equivalent to three-month salary of a typesetter) and typesetters had to rely on individual funding to learn computer skills. Although the scenario has improved lately with the establishment of several affordable vocational training institutes, psychological rehabilita- tion is still an overlooked issue as mental health does not receive adequate attention in Bangladesh. Our interviewees reported that many typesetters never fully recovered from the trauma of being dislodged. Recent HCI for Development (HCI4D) work on mental health in the Global South has proposed a path forward that can be incorporated to support the workers for future technology-induced job displacements [69]. Our results show that the typesetters who could not cope with the new technology eventually returned to their home villages. Their rural birthplaces acted as the safety valves but with the gradual urbanization of the country and for large-scale blue-collar job displacements, this will not be a solution. According to the World Bank, social safety net programs in Bangladesh have been immensely successful to reduce poverty by supporting a range of population groups – old-aged people, widowed, deserted, and re- pressed women, disabled people and transgender people – through different types of assistance [11]. In the current fiscal year, almost 155 Technology Adoption Dynamics of the Press Workers in Bangladesh COMPASS ’20, June 15–17, 2020, , Ecuador 1.5 million additional people have come under the social safety net coverage, which brings the total number of beneficiaries to almost eight million [10]. We argue that to counter against the upcoming large-scale technology-induced job displacement, enhancing the social safety net programs to incorporate this new group of vul- nerable people into existing initiatives such as Vulnerable Group Development (VGD), Vulnerable Group Feeding (VGF), and Food for Work might be helpful as these have already been demonstrated to be effective programs [10, 11]. 6.3 Opportunities in the gig economy The gig economy in Bangladesh might play an important role to reshape the careers of these unemployed workers. According to a recent report from the cross-border payment platform Payoneer, Bangladesh is in the list of top ten fastest growing freelance mar- kets in the world [13]. This suggests that Bangladesh already has a good work-from-home platform for technologically advanced younger gig economy enthusiasts. This ecosystem should be ex- tended to include people who are not technologically adept but can still contribute their physical labor to the gig economy platforms that offer delivery and ridesharing services. There have been nu- merous start-ups for these services such as Pathao [5], Shohoz [4], eCourier [3], HungryNaki [2], GoGoBangla [1], some of which are environmentally sustainable. For example, eCourier offers a bicycle based delivery service in Dhaka [3], the capital city of Bangladesh. Expanding these platforms might be a useful way to rehabilitate the unemployed workers. At the same time, the issues of ethical labor relations should also be addressed so that these workers are not underpaid, overworked, and constantly monitored [53]. As opportunities in the gig economy keep increasing, new shapes and genres of labor unions and movements are being formed. The HCI community has come forward to support these initiatives by delineating avenues for further research on technology and gig work, especially regarding personal information of workers, algo- rithmic management of platforms, and facilitation of gig works through social media [27, 55]. In her case study of the agreement between a gig economy platform and a labor union in Australia, Kate Minter describes the outcomes of the innovative negotiation between the two parties [63]. Similar works need to be done in the digital platform space in the Global South to ensure full and pro- ductive employment and decent work conditions for gig workers. 6.4 Class identity It can be seen from our findings that the typesetters who were dislodged belonged to an interesting social class. On the one hand, they were not strictly working-class people as they had a higher social status than peons or gatekeepers. On the other hand, they were also not educated enough to hold a white-collar job. Despite a lower salary, they used to live in the bubble of being a white-collar worker as they were involved in the prestigious task of publish- ing newspapers that were read carefully and thoroughly by the members of the upper class society. This ambiguous class identity ultimately resulted in their lack of resistance and voice when it mattered. Initially, when their resistance worked in favor of white- collar workers, they got support from those colleagues. Later, when they were left alone, they failed to continue their protest. To put things into perspective, the rickshaw pullers – a classic example of the working class people – have been facing a serious backlash from the government in the last three decades for caus- ing extreme traffic jams in Dhaka [47]. In fact, when government tried to ban them three decades ago, they used to arrange similar protests like the typesetters. Due to their consolidated class iden- tity and strong sympathy from middle class citizens who are their passengers, they have been able to force the government overturn its decisions for multiple times over the last three decades. This suggests that the relationships between workers and the govern- ment in Bangladesh operates within a broader political context and can get disrupted by strong class identity of the working class and external pressure from the civil society. Additionally, in Bangladesh, the law does not always protect the weak [66]. In the case of type- setters, we have seen that they were often deprived of the legal protection. The process of compensation was very long, which put the poor workers in a precarious condition as they could not afford to continue their legal expenditure and had to leave without proper compensation. All these findings suggest that not all workers will face similar consequences in the future and some will be more adversely affected than others. In a sense, the typesetters we inter- viewed portray an image of the worst case scenario as they failed to receive adequate support from the public, the private companies, and the government mechanisms. Our findings also demonstrate the importance of labor union to protect the right of the workers. In the case of the dislodged typesetters, due to the presence of a strong labor union, they initially had the bargaining chips to negotiate with the owners. It was only when the union was infiltrated and a few leaders were bribed, that the workers started losing their voice along with their negotiation power. The situation has become worse in the last three decades and the workers of the RMG industry have been hit hardest due to the diminishing role of the labor unions. For example, in 2013, the collapse of the Rana Plaza building in Savar – a suburb of Dhaka – resulted in 1,134 fatalities [6]. It has been reported that on the morning of the collapse, workers refused to go inside the building as large cracks had appeared in the building the day before, but they were compelled to enter the building and continue working [6]. Tragedies of this type can be prevented if labor unions can function effectively. 6.5 Participatory design In addition to policy recommendations, our findings also generate some design implications. We argue that the precarity of workers should be made more central to the design process in HCI by en- gaging more with institutions and grassroots communities [79]. Events such as civic hackathons can be leveraged in this regard as they have become popular in Bangladesh in recent years [15]. In their analysis of smart city projects, Lodato and DiSalvo identify administrative gap as an institutional constraint that limits the ef- fectiveness of participatory design events such as hackathons [59]. The end result of a government-sponsored hackathon is usually a demonstration that a prototype has merit, without much guideline on how and by whom it will be managed eventually [59]. As a result of this administrative gap, a hackathon fails to achieve its in- tended goal. To work within institutions in Bangladesh and finding 156 COMPASS ’20, June 15–17, 2020, , Ecuador Syed Faiz Ahmed, Pratyasha Saha, and S M Taiabul Haque pathways around them, this issue of administration gap should be considered in the context of participatory design. We also support the notion of designing for polity and civics [44]. We believe that in addition to a better user experience, designers should also focus on a better citizen experience. Finally, our findings call for community activism beyond protests [34] and highlight the importance of re-thinking the existing social and political affairs to protect more than six million workers in Bangladesh who are vulnerable to technology-induced job displacement in the next two decades. 7 LIMITATIONS AND CONCLUSIONS We acknowledge that the sample size of our study is not very large as typesetting was a very specialized profession. Despite our best efforts, we could manage to find only three retired workers who agreed to talk to us. We note that three other potential participants refused to give us their interviews as they were uncomfortable to talk about the topic. In addition, as there was no female type- setter in that period, we could not capture and report any gender dynamics. Finally, we investigated an urban phenomenon and our interviewees are all urban workers from three decades ago. Despite having theses limitations, our work highlights the impor- tance of critically examining the past technological breakthroughs to prepare for the upcoming ones. economics, the concept of creative destruc- tion is associated with describing the process of downsizing in a company or an industry after a technological breakthrough takes place, where previous skills are deemed unnecessary [73]. the fear of unemployment with the advent of new technology is an ancient phenomenon, which originated since the invention of wheel [82]. many rulers from the past public works that would help them gain popularity and safeguard political power [65]. after the first industrial revolution, however, there has been a paradigm shift in the relationship between technology and political economy [80]. the technological breakthroughs of the industrial revolution replaced rulers and feudal lords of the past age with private industrial capitalists who owned the means of production, including machinery and tools, and they started to gradually re- place human workers with machines for increased productivity and profits. as karl marx pointed out in his “das kapital”, when machines are employed in one branch of industry, it creates a re- dundancy of labor in other branches and results in a sharp fall of wages [61]. friedrich engels – the co-author of marx in the communist manifesto – sharply criticized those industrialists as they ignore the perils of the workers and accumulate wealth on the misery of the mass of wage earners [62]. this trend persisted in the western society during and after the great depression era. beard argued that engineers and scientists are responsible for mass unemployment as their inventions were pivotal behind creating the situation [29]. in his works, zachary demonstrated how the fear of automation returned as businesses began heavily relying on computers for the first time and machine tools slowed job growth on the shop floor [83]. marxist scholar braverman’s famous work on “deskilling” draws attention to the dehumanizing nature of routine work, which, according to his observation, had persisted since the birth of the factory system [35]. our work reflects these arguments in the context of the global south as our participants experienced a similar transition in the wake of computerization in the press industry of bangladesh. 2.2 embracing the new technology according to joseph schumpeter (who originally proposed the con- cept of creative destruction), in a capitalistic society, economic growth oscillates between contractions and expansions. with new innovations, older technologies are disrupted and a monopoly of the new technology is established, resulting in a contraction in the business cycle [73]. however, when more and more entrepreneurs adopt the new technology, it becomes less monopolistic and more competitive, leading to an expansion in the economy. he argues that creative destruction is an important trait of the modern capi- talistic society as it keeps the economic growth running through alternating cycles of contractions and expansions. embracing the new technology, they survived, thrived, or disappeared. to properly understand this issue, it is important to recognize two key social and political factors. first, during this transition period, the job of a typesetter was symbolically important. on the one hand, it required literacy and had the aura of clerical jobs. on the other hand, it involved physical labor and the payment was not significantly higher than the hourly wages of the working class people. as a result, they consolidated as a buffer class with a high level of self- esteem but a low to moderate level of income. second, this transition period coincided with a spell of political turbulence in bangladesh when democratic parties there had been organizing a mass uprising to oust military dictatorship. the strength and the political power of labor unions were very crucial and played an important role in the adoption or negotiation dynamics of these workers. 4 methods our interviewer (also a co-author of the paper) is a (p4) age was another important factor as the older typesetters were in a disadvantageous position to adapt to computers compared to the younger ones because their scope of utilising the new skill was very limited with very few years left in their job. thus, the charisma of computers did not work well for those old, high-school educated typesetters. instead, computer became a curse for them as they gradually started losing their jobs because of it. 5.2 computer is the serpent the stories of the typesetters who were dislodged as a result of tech- nological unemployment portrayed a gloomy image. (p2) according to the veteran street vendors of that area, the demon- strations of typesetters for salvaging their jobs became a regular event. even some government employees who were under the threat of losing their jobs and other benefits joined the processions hiding their identity cards. to counter this, the owners initially tried to exert force by hiring goons who got involved in physical brawls. one participant described us one of those incidents from his memory: “i can clearly recall that day when our nightmare be- came a reality and we finally received the notice of being fired. on that day, i was scheduled for the evening shift and when i reached office, i found that we were not allowed to enter the office premises as some goons were resisting us. we made some verbal and physical alter- cations with them and luckily our fellow workers, who were working in the morning shift, were inside the office and they joined us. we could eventually successfully enter the office that day.” (p1) this maneuvering period continued for a while when hired goons and the local police force were used to crush the resistance of the workers. however, the owners eventually adopted the age-old technique of divide and rule by making a compromise with the union leaders. it was alleged by some of our interviewees that a few union labors made a deal with the owners and acted as a double agent. one of them said: “in our office, a few top union leaders basically back- stabbed us. we were not against computers but we wanted to keep our job safe. these leaders told us that computers would be used experimentally to print only one ‘pata’ (one leaf/both sides of the page), but we were tricked. they started to print four instead of one and the number increased subsequently”. (p5) some of those union leaders were also used as a bargaining agent for golden handshakes. they told the workers that the own- ers would compensate them if they resign. initially, some of the workers accepted the package and were paid. however, when the number of workers got reduced and the protests became weaker, the owners started to provide bounced checks. one of the interviewees mentioned: “you know, these are big companies, no banks dared to touch them even if their checks were bounced. so, the cheated workers could do nothing. i know many people who never received the payment and died with the grief”. (p5) the owners also fired some of the influential union leaders who could not be bribed and when they went to court, they did not receive adequate legal support. the owners, who were politically, socially, and financially very strong, eventually won the legal battles. one interviewee frustratingly said: “this is a third world country, the law book states to save the hapless but it always favors the powerful ones”. (p7) aside from ageism, older typesetters faced a couple of other dis- advantages. first, as they spent more years with the old technology, the effort of re-learning new technology and spending fewer re- maining years of their career in working on it did not appear to be an attractive proposition to them. the alternative option – a golden handshake – appeared more lucrative to them as that came with a promise of a severance package. compared to younger typesetters, the older workers were therefore more interested to retire with the package. however, as mentioned above, the owners basically tricked them by starting to write bounced checks after a while. second, the union law mandated that between two willing work- ers, the golden handshake will be offered in a last-in-first-out pro- cess where the worker with fewer working years would leave first with a smaller amount of severance package. this essentially meant that older typesetters received the bounced checks as they retired later in the order and their misfortunes were the most extreme. 5.6 adopting the new technology having described the challenges and the perils of the displaced workers, we now focus on the success stories of the workers who successfully adopted the new computer technology. one of our in- terviewees described us his journey of becoming a graphic designer for newspapers in the computer era. he said: “our owner was a very kind person. his instruction was to keep the job of all the existing workers to the best of their ability, even after the adaptation of computer. we were given chances and options but unfortunately, many workers failed to grab those as they were intimidated and thought that it would be an impossible task for them to master computer skills with very little formal education. but, i was adamant despite having very little formal education. i gave tremendous effort and time and ultimately that paid rich dividend as i thrived in the same office with my computer skills”. (p1) the responses of other successful workers also echoed the same sentiment. they identified two major factors that facilitated their adaptation: individual perseverance and kindness of the owner. all of them acknowledged that the scenario was chaotic as there was no clear institutional policy and luck played an important role in their survival. they remarked that workers who were not lucky enough to have a reasonable owner simply perished. these participants further described their long path to adapta- tion. one of them said: i used to get 4,000 bdt2 per month. my house rent was 2,000 bdt and i had to pay 12,000 bdt for a six-month long computer course. luckily, i had the chance to pay in monthly installments but after paying 2,000 bdt per month for the training, i did not have a single penny and had to borrow from my friends and relatives in those months. however, many of my colleagues did not have this opportunity of spending 2,000 bdt per month as they had to feed their family members”. (p1) he also acknowledged that adapting to computer is a lifelong learning process: “the main challenge was keeping up with the pace of technology. i realized that the styles, techniques, and software were ever changing. some were very enthusi- astic and learned the basic function of computers but failed to learn new things continuously. ironically, when i started learning complex yet more effective software and other techniques in computer, my dynamic col- leagues – who successfully passed the stumble block of technological transformation from hot-metal (typeset- ting) to computer – started to feel that the technology is here to stay as a stationary phenomenon. in my opinion, people who are always ready to sail with the wind of change will survive and thrive in the long run”. job displacements. for example, the ready made garment (rmg) sector, which is many times larger than the press sector in bangladesh, will face a far more serious consequence once automation starts destroying millions of jobs in that sector [7, 8, 26]. in fact, there are a number of striking similarities between today’s conditions and conditions from three decades ago. the owners of the garments today are just as powerful as the owners of the newspaper corporations from that period were, as they hold a similar political and cabinet power [12]. in addition, labor unions are politically infiltrated and multiple banks are on the verge of collapse [8, 9, 42]. the power asymmetry between owners and workers is far more pronounced now compared to three decades ago. without advanced planning and proper intervention from the government, there will be an unprecedented level of panic once automation affects the rmg sector. one important strategy would be educating workers about the potential consequences of industry 4.0 and ai invasions in their life. building on existing hci research on sms, ivr, and participatory video based interventions for education and awareness [56, 81], we suggest that the government reaches out to the general people. at the same time, there should be social awareness and protest against marginalization of the workers. the lessons learned from existing hci and ictd work around social movement might be helpful in this regard [21]. to unite workers and record their grievances, citizen-journalism has been demonstrated to be an effective tech- nique in the global south. communal surveillance might also be a useful approach to prevent chaos and disorder in extreme cases [84]. as technological change is exponential [57], it is quite natural that future workers might experience multiple technological break- throughs in their lifetime. our findings show that even the workers who managed to cope successfully with the previous technological breakthrough are not mentally prepared to face the forthcoming one. considering their age, it is reasonable that in future they will not be required to face a similar challenge that they successfully overcame almost three decades ago. however, it is highly probable that future workers in the global south will experience multiple cycles of the schumpeterian model of innovation and growth and they have to keep adapting to each of those to survive in the long run [73], which might become a significantly more difficult task. any policy therefore should consider the importance of lifelong learning and the government should incentivize the lifelong learn- ing programs. 6.2 rehabilitating workers the rehabilitation of the workers should be prioritized. as can be seen from our findings, computer training centers were expensive (equivalent to three-month salary of a typesetter) and typesetters had to rely on individual funding to learn computer skills. although the scenario has improved lately with the establishment of several affordable vocational training institutes, psychological rehabilita- tion is still an overlooked issue as mental health does not receive adequate attention in bangladesh. our interviewees reported that many typesetters never fully recovered from the trauma of being dislodged. recent hci for development (hci4d) work on mental health in the global south has proposed a path forward that can be incorporated to support the workers for future technology-induced job displacements [69]. our results show that the typesetters who could not cope with the new technology eventually returned to their home villages. their rural birthplaces acted as the safety valves but with the gradual urbanization of the country and for large-scale blue-collar job displacements, this will not be a solution. according to the world bank, social safety net programs in bangladesh have been immensely successful to reduce poverty by supporting a range of population groups – old-aged people, widowed, deserted, and re- pressed women, disabled people and transgender people – through different types of assistance [11]. in the current fiscal year, almost 155 thoroughly by the members of the upper class society. this ambiguous class identity ultimately resulted in their lack of resistance and voice when it mattered. initially, when their resistance worked Poor Physical Condition The physical condition means a condition of the well-being of a student when all internal and external body parts are functioning well according to the requirement. This value is existing in the rural societies in Bangladesh. Lack of Quality of Education There are some factors which directly influence the quality of education like frequent absenteeism of teachers, long distance of school, poor educational and physical facilities. Income equality is a major influential factor in quality education. There are some factors of inequality in primary education like a misinterpretation of religion, socio-cultural norms, illiterate parents and almost no exception for girl’s study [24]. The social economic class is another factor of poor quality education. Because the upper class of society can manage costly education for their children which is opposite in rural areas of Bangladesh. People having a lower class or lower income group are bound to send their children to the government school or NGO’s school where less attention is paid by the concerned authority for maintaining quality education. D. Economic Hardship Economic hardship is a major problem for school dropout. About 31.5% people are living below the poverty line in Bangladesh. The livelihood is very hard for the people where the price hike of a daily necessary commodity is a regular matter. People are bound to spend their earning for purchasing essential commodity by reducing the cost of education. The study shows that 80% of earning is being used for buying essential food items for livelihood without saving any money at the rural as well as urban areas in Bangladesh [12]. Children are bound to leave their school and join to work for earning money for livelihood. Child labour is a very known word in Bangladesh where about 22.9% of the child labourer is doing life risky jobs to carry on the livelihood of their family. Garments industry of Bangladesh is an experience to get a large number of girl’s enrollment where most of them are school dropout [25]. It usually happens after completion of primary education before completion of primary and secondary education for earning money to maintain the livelihood of their family. E. def most_similar(word, topn=2):
    word = nlp.vocab[str(word)]
    queries = [
        w for w in word.vocab 
        if w.is_lower == word.is_lower and w.prob >= -15 and np.count_nonzero(w.vector)
    ]

    by_similarity = sorted(queries, key=lambda w: word.similarity(w), reverse=True)
    return [(w.lower_,w.similarity(word)) for w in by_similarity[:topn+1] if w.lower_ != word.lower_]

def get_alternative_spellings(list_of_words):
    alt_spellings = []
    
    for word in list_of_words:
        # if word is in the uk_spelling list
        # then save the us_spelling
        temp_list = list(uk_us_spelling_pairs.loc[uk_us_spelling_pairs["uk_spelling"]==word, "us_spelling"])
        if len(temp_list)>0:
            alt_spellings.append(temp_list)
        
        # if the word is in the us_spelling list
        # then save the uk_spelling
        temp_list = list(uk_us_spelling_pairs.loc[uk_us_spelling_pairs["us_spelling"]==word, "uk_spelling"])
        if len(temp_list)>0:
            alt_spellings.append(temp_list)
            
    # flatten list
    alt_spellings = [item for sublist in alt_spellings for item in sublist]
    
    # remove duplicates from the list
    alt_spellings = list(dict.fromkeys(alt_spellings))
    
    # sort list in alphabetical order
    alt_spellings.sort()
    
    return alt_spellings
